The coupling constant
K
Motor constant
0.50
The motor constant links the electrical and mechanical domains. It sets both the torque produced per amp of current (T = K·i) and the back-EMF voltage generated per unit of rotational speed (V_emf = K·ω). A larger K means a stronger motor that produces more torque per amp — but it also generates more back-EMF, which acts like a brake and limits top speed at a given voltage. This single number is the bridge between the circuit and the shaft.

What's driving each metric
Cost
—
Driven by material quantity and precision. Low R needs thick copper wire. High K needs strong magnets (often rare-earth). High L needs more winding turns. Low b (smooth, low-friction) requires precision bearings and tighter tolerances.
Weight / size
—
High J directly means a larger, heavier rotor. High L means more winding material — more copper wire wound on a bigger core. Low R also requires thicker wire, adding mass. These three parameters together determine how large and heavy the motor is.
Torque output
—
Stall torque = K · V / R. A high K produces more torque per amp. A low R allows more current to flow, increasing torque further. These two parameters together set the motor's peak force capability. J and b do not affect stall torque directly.
Top speed
—
No-load speed = K · V / (R·b + K²). A larger K increases torque but also generates more back-EMF, which acts as a self-imposed speed limit. Lower R and lower b both allow the motor to spin faster at a given voltage. There is a direct tension between high torque (high K) and high speed.
Response speed
—
How quickly the motor reaches its target speed. Governed by both time constants: τ_e = L/R (electrical) and τ_m = J/b (mechanical). A large J or large L slows the response. High R and high b speed it up — but at the cost of torque, efficiency, and top speed respectively.
Efficiency
—
The fraction of electrical input power converted to mechanical output at steady state. R dissipates energy as heat in the windings — the main loss mechanism. High K reduces the current needed for a given torque, lowering resistive losses. Low b reduces friction losses at the shaft.
